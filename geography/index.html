💧 Earth's Life Support Systems
The water and carbon cycles are fundamental to life on Earth. They regulate climate, support ecosystems, and are increasingly disrupted by human activity. Understanding their stores, flows, and feedback loops is essential for Paper 1, Section B.
💧 The Water Cycle
The hydrological cycle moves water between the atmosphere, land surface, and oceans through evaporation, condensation, precipitation, and runoff. It is a closed system — no water enters or leaves Earth.
Key stores
Oceans
96.5% of all water
Ice caps/glaciers
1.74% — shrinking
Groundwater
1.69% — aquifers
Atmosphere
0.001% — rain, cloud
Key flows
☀️
Evapotranspiration
Solar energy converts liquid water to vapour from surfaces and plants. Largest transfer in the cycle.
🌧️
Precipitation
Water vapour condenses and falls as rain, snow, or hail. Drives river systems and groundwater recharge.
🏔️
Surface runoff
Water flows over impermeable surfaces into rivers. Increased by urbanisation and deforestation.
🌊
Infiltration & throughflow
Water soaks into soil and moves slowly through the ground to rivers and aquifers.
🌿 The Carbon Cycle
Carbon moves between the atmosphere, biosphere, hydrosphere, and lithosphere. Human emissions have significantly increased atmospheric CO₂, from 280 ppm pre-industrial to 422 ppm in 2023.
Key stores
Lithosphere
100,000,000 Gt — fossil fuels, rocks
Oceans
38,000 Gt — largest active store
Soil/permafrost
2,300 Gt — at risk from warming
Atmosphere
860 Gt — rising rapidly
Key flows & human disruption
🌿
Photosynthesis
Plants absorb ~120 Gt CO₂/year from the atmosphere. Deforestation reduces this natural sink.
🔥
Combustion (human)
Burning fossil fuels releases ~36 Gt CO₂/year — carbon stored over millions of years released in decades.
🌊
Ocean uptake
Oceans absorb ~25% of human CO₂ emissions, but this increases ocean acidification.
🧊
Permafrost feedback
Thawing permafrost releases stored methane and CO₂ — a dangerous positive feedback loop.
Human impacts on both cycles
Deforestation
Reduces interception and transpiration (water cycle). Removes carbon sink and releases stored carbon (carbon cycle). Increases surface runoff and flood risk.
Urbanisation
Impermeable surfaces increase runoff and reduce infiltration (water cycle). Urban heat islands intensify evaporation. Higher energy use increases carbon emissions.
Agriculture
Irrigation disrupts local water cycles. Livestock produce methane. Soil tillage releases stored carbon. Wetland drainage removes carbon sinks.
Climate change feedback
Warming increases evaporation (intensifying the water cycle). Melting permafrost releases CO₂/methane. Reduced snow cover lowers albedo, amplifying warming.
OCR H481 — Paper 1, Section B: Earth's Life Support Systems
Students must understand the water and carbon cycles as systems — their stores, flows, and the feedback mechanisms that link them. Key themes: how human activity is disrupting both cycles; the role of the oceans and biosphere as carbon sinks; and how these disruptions link to climate change. The relationship between these cycles and life on Earth is the unifying concept.
🏙️ Changing Spaces, Making Places
Places are not fixed — they are constantly being made and remade by economic, social, and cultural forces. This section explores how places change, how people experience and represent them, and what drives regeneration or decline. The spec asks: what makes a place? How do they change? Who has the power to shape them?
Theories of place change
Deindustrialisation
The decline of manufacturing in the late 20th century transformed industrial cities. Detroit lost 60% of its population; Sheffield and Manchester had to reinvent themselves around services and culture.
Gentrification
Higher-income groups move into historically working-class areas, raising property values and displacing existing communities. Seen in Brixton, London and Hackney. A contested process.
Regeneration strategies
Urban Development Corporations, Enterprise Zones, and culture-led regeneration (e.g. Bilbao's Guggenheim effect) are used to attract investment to declining areas. Success is debated.
Sense of place
Geographers study how places develop meaning and identity. Place identity is shaped by who has the power to represent a place — through marketing, art, media, and planning decisions.

✈️ Global Connections: Human Rights & Migration
Global migration is one of the defining issues of the 21st century. People move for economic opportunity, to flee conflict or persecution, or because of environmental change. The governance of migration — and the protection of human rights — involves complex tensions between national sovereignty and international obligations.
Human rights framework
Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948)
Establishes the right to seek asylum, the right to a nationality, and freedom from persecution. Non-binding but forms the basis of international refugee law.
1951 Refugee Convention
Defines who is a refugee and their rights. States cannot return refugees to countries where they face serious threats (non-refoulement). Does not cover climate migrants — a major gap.
UN Global Compact for Migration (2018)
First global framework to address international migration comprehensively. Non-binding. Aims to make migration safer and more orderly. US, Hungary, and others refused to sign.
Climate migration — the gap
Climate migrants have no legal protection under international law. By 2050, 216 million people may be internally displaced by climate change. This is the major emerging human rights challenge.

⚠️ Hazard Management Decision Simulator
You are the emergency management coordinator for a region facing an imminent M7.0 earthquake. A series of decisions must be made. Choose wisely — each decision affects lives, costs, and long-term resilience.
Hazard management strategies
Prediction & monitoring
Seismometers, GPS ground deformation, radon gas sensors. Japan's early warning system gives 30-90 seconds notice. Cannot prevent but reduces casualties.
Building design
Base isolators, cross-bracing, flexible frames. Japan's strict building codes saved thousands in 2011. Haiti's weak buildings turned M7.0 into 300,000 deaths.
Land use planning
Restricting development in high-risk zones. Exclusion zones around volcanoes. Coastal setback lines for tsunami risk. Politically difficult but highly effective.
Emergency response
Evacuation plans, stockpiled supplies, trained response teams. New Zealand's GeoNet system. The first 72 hours are critical for survival of trapped victims.

🦠 Disease Dilemmas
Disease patterns are shaped by physical environments, social inequality, economic development, and governance. The key debate: are diseases natural phenomena or the product of human choices and social conditions? Students must distinguish between communicable and non-communicable diseases and evaluate the role of different factors in their spread and management.
Key debate questions (Paper 3 essay focus)
"Disease is fundamentally a product of poverty"
Evaluate: tuberculosis, cholera, and malaria disproportionately kill the poor. But non-communicable diseases now kill more in LEDCs too. Is development always the answer?
Cultural factors & NCDs
Diet, smoking, alcohol, and physical activity are culturally influenced. How far are non-communicable diseases a product of choice vs. structural inequality and corporate behaviour?
Global governance of disease
WHO coordinates responses — but COVID revealed its limitations. Vaccine nationalism vs. COVAX. How effective is international cooperation when national interests conflict?
OCR H481 — Paper 3, Topic 3.2: Disease Dilemmas
Students must understand the global distribution of communicable and non-communicable diseases, the factors that influence their spread (physical, social, economic, cultural, political), and the effectiveness of different management strategies. The debate centres on whether disease patterns reflect natural geography or human inequality — and what interventions are most effective and equitable.
