Cyclical unemployment
1.2%
Caused by a fall in aggregate demand — when the economy shrinks, firms cut output and lay off workers. It rises sharply in recessions and falls in booms. Also called demand-deficient unemployment.
💊 Policy: Demand stimulus (fiscal or monetary)
Structural unemployment
1.8%
Caused by a mismatch between workers' skills and available jobs, often following industrial change. The decline of UK coal, steel, and textiles in the 1980s created mass structural unemployment. Automation creates new structural unemployment today.
💊 Policy: Retraining, education, regional policy
Frictional unemployment
1.2%
The natural unemployment that exists even in a healthy economy — people between jobs, searching for better opportunities. Some frictional unemployment is healthy and inevitable. The duration of job search depends on information, mobility, and benefits.
💊 Policy: Job centres, information, mobility

Edexcel A Level Economics — Unemployment
The three main types of unemployment require different policy responses: cyclical unemployment responds to demand management (interest rate cuts, fiscal stimulus); structural unemployment requires supply-side policies (retraining, education, regional regeneration); frictional unemployment is reduced by better labour market information and mobility. The NAIRU (Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate of Unemployment) is the equilibrium rate at which inflation is stable — attempting to push unemployment below it causes accelerating inflation (the Phillips Curve).
